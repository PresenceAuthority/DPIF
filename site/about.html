About The Presence Authority

Built by people who
saw what was missing
and decided to fix it themselves.

The Presence Authority was founded when its co-founder encountered AI digital twin technology being deployed without transparency, without consent, and without any governance — and realised nobody was building the standard to change that.

The Origin Story

The moment that made
everything clear.

"I looked for the standard that said how you create a digital representation of a real person ethically. It didn't exist. So we built it."

— Bobbie-Jane Skewes, Co-Founder & CEO

Bobbie-Jane Skewes spent over two decades in luxury hospitality — pre-opening resorts across Southeast Asia, leading commercial teams across nine countries, protecting the identity and integrity of some of the world's most prestigious brands. In that world, brand standards were not optional. They existed to protect everyone in the relationship.

Then she began working with an AI digital twin company — and discovered that the standards she had taken for granted simply did not exist in this space.

Her co-founder Brad Gaylard had spent a decade in edtech — building structured educational frameworks at national scale in Australia. When Bobbie-Jane described what she was seeing, he immediately recognised the problem: a field that had outpaced its governance, and needed a framework rigorous enough to be authoritative but practical enough to actually be used.

Together, they built DPIF.

The Founding Moment
What Bobbie-Jane encountered working with an AI avatar company changed everything. The company was presenting their digital twin technology as proprietary — their own platform, their own software. In reality, they were using third-party technology without disclosing this to clients. The people whose faces, voices, and likenesses were being replicated had no idea how that replication was actually being built, stored, or controlled.

The handover of completed digital twins had no governance process. No consent documentation. No defined use boundaries. No accountability if something went wrong. No plan for what happened to the twin when the relationship ended.

The question that followed was direct: how do you protect the person on the other side of this process?

There was no answer — because there was no standard. So Bobbie-Jane and Brad built one.

DPIF was not built in a research lab or a policy committee. It was built by two practitioners who had seen the problem from the inside — and who understood that the people being represented by AI needed someone to stand up for their right to identity, consent, and dignity.

Why Governance Matters

When a person's face, voice, and communicative presence can be replicated and operated at scale, the question is no longer whether the technology is impressive. The question is whether the person behind the representation retains any meaningful control over their own identity.

Why This Matters Now

The technology is here.
The governance is not.

AI avatars, voice clones, and digital twins are being deployed at scale across enterprise, media, healthcare, and finance. Real people's identities are being replicated and operated with minimal oversight, unclear consent, and no accountability structures.

Existing regulation does not address the specific governance needs of digital representations of real people. The EU AI Act is a start — but it does not go far enough. That gap is what DPIF fills.

The Presence Authority is the only organisation in the world that publishes and certifies against a standard specifically designed for this problem.
